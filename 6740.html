  General Notes:

Note. Cedarville, Warren Co., Virginia is an unincorporated populated place in Warren Co. It is about 16 miles from Upperville, VA, where his cousins Charles Van Brent Cushman and his wife lived and are buried. [JAC, Feb, 2015].
EC Cushman's Washington DC death certificate gives Cedarville, Warren Co, as his place of death. On the other hand, the certificate was issued in the District of Columbia (no death certificates were required in Virginia).
No sanatorium has been found in that era in Cedarville. Although the town had a fair number of boarding establishments, none seem to have been dedicated to the sick.
During that era Virginia operated the important Catawba Sanatorium, but it was just outside Roanoke. Another possible sanatorium flourished in Charlottesville.
It seems likely that Allerton Seward Cushman, Edwins' brother, may have arranged for Edwin's removal to Cedarville in late 1906 or early 1907, until his death in September. Allison then filed a death certificate and burial permit in DC because he lived there at the time, and because Virginia had no comparable formal process.

"Edwin C. Cushman, Jr., nicknamed "Carlino" was an adventurer. He left his family to participate in the Alaska Gold Rush. Subsequently he discovered a tribe of hitherto unknown Indians in Colorado, some of whom he brought to the World's Fair in St. Louis in 1898. From them he contracted tuberculosis from which he died." (p. 209, C.VB Cushman).

In fact, prior to his participation in the Gold Rush, Cushman worked as a cowhand and semi-professional on various archeological digs in the Southwest from 1893-1897. Thereafter he was hired in 1903 by William McGee of the Department of Anthropology of the Louisiana Purchase Exposition to bring members of the Siri tribe to the Exposition, and after that failed, the Cocopa from Baja California. He escorted Cocopa individuals to St. Louis in April 1904. By late July he had resigned his position due to tuberculosis and entered a sanatorium in Saranac Lake, NY.

Placermining Grants Vol. 3 (19,259 records)
The third of three "Placerming Grants Volumes."
After gold was discovered in 1896 on Bonanza Creek, thousands of claims were staked along tributaries of the Yukon and Klondike Rivers. The Yukon Gold Commissioner's Office issued "Placermining Grants," enabling prospectors to become miners and work their claims for "placer" findings (mineral deposits containing gold, left by glaciers or rivers ). This, the first of three Placermining Grants volumes, lists almost 20,000 numbered grants and the names of their owners

On November 8, 1901 Alice M. Cushman sued "Edward" C. Cushman for divorce in St. Louis, Missouri, seeking also custody of their 10 year daughter, Violet. The judgment on Dec. 30, 1901 was by default. Violet alleged that Edwin had been gone for more than a year, that he failed to provide for his family, and that they had ceased living together in February 1898, a few months before he entered the Yukon territory.

1910 Census entry for Violet Cushman Coddington give place of birth of father as Missouri rather than Rome. Also, no statewide or county death certificates were mandatory in Virginia between 1896 and 1912. A death certificate was issued on Sept. 30, 1907, in the District of Columbia. He was cremated on 30 Sept. 1907.
